Introduction  
RNA  sequencing  provides  a  rich  and  biologically  grounded  layer  of  information  for  biomarker  discovery  
and
 
patient
 
selection
 
because
 
it
 
captures
 
transcript
 
abundance,
 
pathway
 
activity,
 
cell-state
 
composition,
 
and
 molecular  heterogeneity  across  thousands  of  genes  simultaneously  [1].  Although  proteins  and  metabolites  are  often  more  proximal  readouts  of  cellular  phenotype,  proteomic  and  metabolomic  assays  usually  
provide
 
lower
 
molecular
 
coverage
 
and
 
may
 
miss
 
transcript-level
 
diversity
 
that
 
is
 
relevant
 
for
 
disease
 biology  [2].  In  particular,  RNA-seq  can  reveal  transcript  isoforms,  alternative  splicing,  low-abundance  transcripts,  and  sequence-level  evidence  for  unannotated  or  patient-specific  coding  events  that  may  not  be  captured  by  standard  proteomic  profiling  [3].  
This  information  is  especially  important  in  cancer  immunotherapy,  where  treatment  response  and  toxicity  
are
 
shaped
 
by
 
a
 
combination
 
of
 
germline
 
variation,
 
acquired
 
somatic
 
alterations,
 
tumor-intrinsic
 
biology,
 immune-cell  states,  and  patient-specific  inflammatory  programs  [4,  5].  Patient-specific  proteomes  reconstructed  from  RNA-seq  therefore  offer  an  opportunity  to  move  beyond  canonical  reference  
annotations
 
and
 
to
 
characterize
 
expressed
 
protein
 
sequence
 
space
 
in
 
a
 
way
 
that
 
may
 
better
 
reflect
 individual  tumor  biology,  immune  responsiveness,  and  risk  of  immune-related  adverse  events  [6,  7].
 
Cancer  cells  accumulate  genomic  and  transcriptomic  alterations  that  can  generate  patient-specific  protein  
products,
 
including
 
mutant,
 
SNP-containing,
 
truncated,
 
or
 
otherwise
 
noncanonical
 
proteoforms.
 
These
 
altered
 
proteins
 
are
 
usually
 
considered
 
mainly
 
as
 
potential
 
neoantigens,
 
but
 
they
 
may
 
also
 
differ
 
from
 canonical  proteins  in  their  intrinsic  aggregation  and  amyloid-forming  propensity  [8].  Since  amyloidogenicity  is  strongly  influenced  by  amino-acid  sequence,  mutations  or  truncations  may  increase  
hydrophobicity,
 
β-
aggregation
 
propensity,
 
or
 
exposure
 
of
 
aggregation-prone
 
regions,
 
thereby
 
changing
 
the
 proteostatic  burden  of  cancer  cells.  [9]  
In  this  framework,  cancer  genomic  instability  may  produce  a  pool  of  altered  proteins  that  simultaneously  
contributes
 
to
 
immune
 
recognition
 
and
 
proteostasis
 
stress.
 
Mutations
 
may
 
attract
 
immune
 
responses
 
by
 
generating
 
neoepitopes,
 
but
 
they
 
may
 
also
 
reduce
 
tumor
 
cell
 
viability
 
through
 
misfolding
 
or
 
irreversible
 
intracellular
 
aggregation.
 
Therefore,
 
tumor
 
cell
 
death
 
during
 
immune
 
activation
 
may
 
reflect
 
not
 
only
 
enhanced
 
recognition
 
by
 
cytotoxic
 
immune
 
cells,
 
but
 
also
 
increased
 
vulnerability
 
caused
 
by
 
toxic
 
aggregation-prone
 
protein
 
products.
 
Amyloid-prone  proteins  are  closely  linked  to  proteostasis  pathways,  including  chaperone  activity,  ER  stress,  the  unfolded  protein  response,  proteasomal  degradation,  and  autophagy.  [10,  11]  When  these  systems  are  overloaded,  misfolded  or  aggregation-prone  proteins  can  promote  cellular  stress  and  
inflammatory
 
signaling.
 
Conversely,
 
inflammatory
 
cytokines
 
can
 
further
 
aggravate
 
proteostasis
 
imbalance,
 
creating
 
a
 
feed-forward
 
loop
 
between
 
altered
 
protein
 
products,
 
protein
 
misfolding,
 
and
 
inflammation.
 


===== PAGE 4 =====
Inflammation  is  also  a  key  driver  of  systemic  amyloidogenesis.  The  acute-phase  protein  serum  amyloid  A  
is
 
largely
 
regulated
 
by
 
inflammation-associated
 
cytokines,
 
and
 
high
 
circulating
 
SAA
 
concentration
 
is
 
a
 marker  of  acute  and  chronic  inflammatory  states  [12].   SAA  can  also  be  synthesized  in  extrahepatic  tissues,  including  human  carcinoma  metastases  and  cancer  cell  lines,  where  it  may  reflect  tumor  growth  or  the  host  inflammatory  response  [13].  Sustained  inflammatory  stimulation  and  increased  SAA  availability  may  therefore  create  a  permissive  environment  for  amyloid  nucleation  and  deposition.   
Immune  checkpoint  inhibitor  therapy  may  intensify  this  process  by  enhancing  antitumor  immunity  while  
also
 
increasing
 
the
 
risk
 
of
 
immune-related
 
adverse
 
events.
 
irAEs
 
may
 
arise
 
through
 
cross-reactive
 
T-cell
 
responses,
 
epitope
 
spreading,
 
dendritic-cell
 
activation,
 
B-cell
 
activation,
 
autoantibody
 
production,
 
and
 inflammatory  damage  to  normal  tissues  [14].  Importantly,  irAEs  may  not  result  solely  from  ICI-induced  inflammation;  amyloidogenic  protein  products  or  amyloid-like  seeds  may  themselves  act  as  endogenous  danger  signals  and  contribute  to  immune  activation  [15].  
Thus,  the  proposed  idea  suggests  that  amyloidogenicity  may  represent  a  mechanistic  bridge  between  
cancer-associated
 
altered
 
protein
 
products,
 
proteostasis
 
failure,
 
inflammation,
 
and
 
ICI-related
 
toxicity
 
(Figure
 
1).
 
In
 
patients
 
with
 
a
 
high
 
patient-specific
 
amyloidogenic
 
burden,
 
ICI-induced
 
immune
 
activation
 
may
 
amplify
 
inflammatory
 
pathways,
 
increase
 
SAA-driven
 
amyloidogenic
 
pressure,
 
and
 
promote
 
local
 
or
 
systemic
 
amyloid
 
nucleation.
 
Although
 
clinical
 
reports
 
of
 
amyloidosis
 
associated
 
with
 
ICI
 
use
 
remain
 
limited,
 
they
 
support
 
the
 
plausibility
 
of
 
this
 
connection
 
and
 
justify
 
systematic
 
investigation
 
of
 amyloidogenic  burden  as  a  potential  marker  of  irAE  risk  [16,  17,  18].  
Overall,  this  hypothesis  reframes  patient-specific  amyloidogenic  burden  as  a  potential  mediator  linking  
genomic
 
instability,
 
altered
 
proteomes,
 
proteostasis
 
stress,
 
innate
 
inflammation,
 
and
 
immune-related
 
adverse
 
events.
 
Identifying
 
this
 
link
 
may
 
improve
 
patient
 
stratification,
 
help
 
explain
 
heterogeneity
 
in
 
ICI
 
toxicity,
 
and
 
support
 
more
 
individualized
 
management
 
of
 
checkpoint
 
inhibitor
 
therapy.
 
Together,  these  observations  highlight  the  need  to  investigate  biomarkers  that  integrate  amyloidogenic  
potential
 
with
 
immune
 
reactivity
 
in
 
cancer
 
patients
 
receiving
 
immunotherapy.
 
Several
 
related
 
computational
 
approaches
 
have
 
already
 
demonstrated
 
the
 
value
 
of
 
patient-specific
 
sequence
 
information.
 
Proteogenomic
 
workflows
 
can
 
generate
 
individualized
 
protein
 
databases
 
from
 
genomic
 
or
 
transcriptomic
 data  to  improve  detection  of  non-canonical  proteoforms  [19,  20,  21,  22],  while  neoantigen  prediction  pipelines  prioritize  patient-specific  mutant  peptides  with  potential  relevance  for  immune  recognition.  
However,
 
these
 
approaches
 
are
 
primarily
 
focused
 
on
 
proteome
 
annotation,
 
mass-spectrometry
 
search
 
space
 
expansion,
 
or
 
antigen
 
presentation,
 
rather
 
than
 
on
 
the
 
systematic
 
quantification
 
of
 
amyloidogenic
 
burden.
 
To
 
address
 
this
 
gap,
 
we
 
developed
 
Amylice,
 
a
 
reproducible
 
RNA-seq-based
 
proteogenomic
 
workflow
 
designed
 
to
 
generate
 
patient-specific
 
protein
 
sequence
 
spaces
 
and
 
quantify
 
expression-weighted
 
amyloidogenicity.

1.  Pipeline  creation  
Amylice  was  designed  to  move  beyond  gene-  or  transcript-level  expression  analysis  toward  
sequence-aware,
 
sample-specific
 
proteome
 
reconstruction.
 
The

RNA-seq
 
FASTQ

or

transcript
 
expression
 
estimates
 
and
 
filtered

calls.
 
In

full
 
workflow,
 
RNA-seq
 
reads
 
undergo
 
quality
 
control,
 
expression
 
quantification,
 
alignment,
 
and
 
variant
 
calling.
 
In
 
the
 
prepared-input
 
workflow,
 
Amylice
 
starts
 
from
 
expression
 
tables,
 
filtered
 
VCF
 
files,
 
a
 
reference
 
genome
 
sequence,
 
and
 
transcript
 
annotation.
 
These
 
inputs
 
are
 
used
 
to
 
identify
 
expressed
 
coding
 
transcripts
 
and
 
reconstruct
 
protein
 
sequences
 
that
 
are
 
plausible
 
for
 
each
 
patient
 
or
 
sample.
 
The  resulting  patient-specific  proteome  is  distinct  from  a  static  reference  proteome.  A  reference  proteome  
represents
 
the
 
expected
 
canonical
 
protein
 
sequence
 
set,
 
whereas
 
the
 
Amylice
 
proteome
 
represents
 
the
 
expressed
 
protein
 
sequence
 
space
 
of
 
an
 
individual
 
sample.
 
It
 
incorporates
 
transcripts
 
that
 
pass
 
expression
 
and
 
coding-sequence
 
filters,
 
haplotype-specific
 
translated
 
proteins,
 
SNP-containing
 
proteins,
 
nonsense-truncated
 
proteins,
 
and
 
indel-derived
 
frameshift
 
products
 
where
 
supported
 
by
 
the
 
variant
 
calls.
 
Thus,

reconstructed
 
proteome
 
captures
 
both
 
what
 
is
 
expressed
 
in

sample
 
and
 
which
 
sequence
 
alterations
 
may
 
modify
 
the
 
encoded
 
protein
 
products.
 
Expression  information  is  used  to  define  the  expressed  coding  transcript  set  before  protein  reconstruction.  
Transcripts
 
below

expression

or
 
with
 
unsuitable

are
 
excluded

reconstructed
 
proteome.
 
Each
 
reconstructed
 
sequence
 
can

to
 
an

or

to

When

or

The  study  does  not  propose  a  clinically  validated  toxicity  biomarker,  but  rather  a  reproducible

The

nonsense-truncated

expression-weighted
 
sum
 
or

of
 
amyloid-prone

The

several

Amylice  demonstrated  the  technical  feasibility  of  calculating  RNA-derived  amyloidogenic  proteome

sequence
